## Galton's Height Dataset

This is Galton's original height dataset used in Data 8. He collected height measurements from 205 families (934 children total) at his Anthropometric Laboratory during the 1884 International Health Exhibition in London. Visitors were paid three pence to be measure, and as such, the data represents a self-selected convenience sample of middle and upper class white English families with the free time and cultural inclination to visit a eugenic exhibition in England. Working-class families, non-white populations, or anyone outside the London area are thus absent. Galton's express goal of collecting and using this data was to assert that physical traits are heritable in predictable ways, and thereby justify selective breeding. Furthermore, he"transmuted" all female heights by multiplying them by 1.08 to convert them to male equivalents, and in doing so, effectively erasing sex-based differences; treating male bodies as the default.

The dataset is freely available as a CSV across the internet. We are using Kaggle's CSV which was originally from the Harvard Dataverse. The widespread availability of a dataset built to justify racial hierarchy as a canonical teaching tool in statistics courses worldwide is telling. We further assert that it is almost always stripped of its ideological origin. For our curriculum, this is the main point, alongside a basic introduction to data work in Jupyter and python. Students will work with the same data Galton used, compute the same regression, and discover how his own results (regression to the mean) ultimately undermine his eugenic thesis. The dataset's racial homogeneity, class bias, and eugenic purpose are entirely the point.

## California Forced Sterilization Records

The data we will be using in our sterilization unit originate from standardized recommendation forms generated by eleven California state institutions (hospitals and homes for the "feebleminded") between 1919 and 1952, during which the state performed approximately 20,000 compulsory sterilizations (one-third of all such procedures in the United States). The original paper records were microfilmed in the 1970s and sat neglected until historian Alexandra Minna Stern discovered the nineteen reels in her filing cabinet at the California Department of Mental Health in 2007. Her team at the University of Michigan's Sterilization and Social Justice Lab spent three years manually digitizing approximately 20,000 records into a database with 212 coded variables per individual.

The raw data is not publicly available for good reason: an estimated 831 survivors were still living as of 2016. California's AB 1764 (2021) established a reparations program with explicit confidentiality protections, and the records contain deeply sensitive personal health information. We will work instead from published summary statistics in two open-access *American Journal of Public Health* papers: Novak et al. (2018), which uses Poisson regression to show that Latina women faced 59% higher sterilization risk than non-Latina counterparts, and Stern et al. (2017), which documents that 68% of estimated living survivors were sterilized as minors. 

Included in this data are individuals institutionalized by state facilities and recommended for sterilization. Sterilizations performed in prisons, private facilities, and county hospitals are not, meaning the true scale of the program is certainly larger than the records capture. The original forms did not systematically record race or ethnicity. Thus, researchers used 1980 Census Spanish-surname lists as a proxy for Latino identity, and acknowledge the imperfection in doing so.

For the purposes of our curriculum, the data restriction is a lesson itself. Students encounter a situation where transparency and accountability exist in a genuine tension with survivor privacy and dignity. This live data justice dilemma shows how published statistics allow us to learn important truths without compromising the people the data describes.

## W.E.B. Du Bois's 1900 Paris Exposition Visualizations

Hand-drawn data visualizations created by W.E.B. Du Bois and his students at Atlanta University for the 1900 Exposition Universelle in Paris will be used in our final unit. The underlying data was drawn from 1790–1890 U.S. Census records, Du Bois's sociological fieldwork, Georgia state and county records, and empirical data collected through what Du Bois called "insider citizen researchers;" members of Black communities across the US who collected data about their own communities. This makes the Du Bois plates a powerful example of data collected *by* marginalized communities *about themselves*, countering racial science that dominated the same exposition.

High-resolution scans are freely available through the Library of Congress digital collection, and reconstructed CSV datasets for approximately thirty plates have been compiled by Anthony Starks through the annual #DuBoisChallenge, which we will be using. These CSVs were created by manually reading off Du Bois's hand-drawn charts, introducing some estimation error, but we feel they are sufficient for students to recreate the visualizations in python.

The plates focus heavily on Georgia, underrepresenting Black life in the North and West. They emphasize a progress narrative; rising literacy, growing property ownership, economic advancement. This is a deliberate rhetorical strategy against claims of Black inferiority, but may still reinforce the narrative that marginalized communities must "prove" their worth through metrics legible to power. The visualizations do not capture lynching, voter suppression, or daily racial terror. Du Bois created them for a European audience, meaning the data was shaped by the politics of who he was trying to persuade. These tensions between data as liberation and data as performance for a white gaze are precisely the kinds of critical tensions our curriculum asks students to sit with.
