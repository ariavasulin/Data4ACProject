# California Forced Sterilization Records: Data Research

*Research compiled 2026-03-18 for Data 4AC Final Project proposal*

---

## 1. Is the Raw Dataset Publicly Available?

**No. The raw individual-level dataset is NOT publicly available.**

The Sterilization and Social Justice Lab (SSJL) at the University of Michigan holds a digitized database of ~19,995 sterilization recommendation records (212 coded variables per record) but **does not provide unrestricted access**. Their [FAQ](https://www.ssjlab.org/faq.html) states they "limit the types of data and records that can be accessed by the general public" citing "responsible data stewardship of sensitive personal information."

**Checked repositories with no results:**
- ICPSR (University of Michigan's own repository)
- Harvard Dataverse
- Dryad
- Zenodo
- Figshare
- GitHub
- Data.gov

**Only published summary statistics** from the AJPH papers are publicly accessible (see Section 7 below).

**To request access:** Submit through [SSJL contact form](https://forms.gle/Sx4AV6rwTUZ7LEvKA) — handled case-by-case, approval not guaranteed.

## 3. Who Created/Collected the Original Data?

**Origin:** Standardized sterilization recommendation forms generated by **California state institutions** from 1919-1952. These were the bureaucratic mechanism through which compulsory sterilizations were authorized.

**Generating institutions (11 total):**

| Type | Institutions |
|------|-------------|
| Homes for the Feebleminded | Sonoma State Home, Pacific Colony (opened 1927) |
| State Hospitals for the Insane | Stockton, Patton, Norwalk, Agnews, Mendocino, Camarillo, Napa, DeWitt, Modesto |

**Scale:** California performed ~20,000 sterilizations — **one-third of all ~60,000 U.S. sterilizations**. By 1921, California accounted for 80% of all U.S. sterilizations.

**What the forms contained:** Patient name, age, sex, institution, family histories, diagnoses, IQ scores, behavioral evaluations, consent documentation, and supplemental letters. All sterilization requests submitted through these forms were approved by the Department of Institutions.

**Archival history:** Original paper files were discarded in the 1970s when the California Department of Mental Health created microfilm copies (19 reels). These reels sat neglected until 2007.

## 4. Who Digitized and Analyzed It?

### The 2007 Discovery
**Alexandra Minna Stern, PhD** (Carroll Smith-Rosenberg Collegiate Professor of American Culture, History, and Women's Studies at University of Michigan) discovered the 19 microfilm reels in a neglected filing cabinet at the California Department of Mental Health in Sacramento while conducting historical research. She had the microfilm duplicated — a critical decision, as the originals were later lost during departmental reorganization following California budget cuts.

### The Sterilization and Social Justice Lab (SSJL)
- **Co-directors:** Alexandra Minna Stern (U Michigan) and Natalie Lira (UIUC)
- **Mission:** Interdisciplinary team studying patterns of eugenic sterilization using mixed methods from social sciences, humanities, and public health
- **Scope:** Initially California, now expanded to Iowa, Michigan, North Carolina, and Utah (~35,000 total records)
- **Website:** [ssjlab.org](https://www.ssjlab.org/)

### Digitization Process
- **Duration:** 3 years of manual data entry
- **Team:** Undergraduate and graduate students at University of Michigan
- **Tool:** REDCap (Research Electronic Data Capture) — HIPAA-compliant secure web application
- **Output:** 212 coded variables per record for ~20,000 sterilization recommendations
- **De-identification:** Created HIPAA-compliant dataset with identifying information removed

## 5. Who Is Included/Excluded? What Variables?

### Dataset Scope
- **Total digitized:** 19,995 sterilization recommendations (1919-1952)
- **With adequate data:** 19,498 individuals
- **Gender split:** 9,586 male (49%), 9,912 female (51%)

### 212 Coded Variables (key ones)
| Variable | Details |
|----------|---------|
| Gender/Sex | Male or Female |
| Age | At sterilization recommendation (range: <12 to ≥50) |
| Race/Ethnicity | **Not systematically recorded** — used proxies (see below) |
| Nativity/Birthplace | US-born, Asian-born (Chinese, Indian, Japanese, Korean, Filipino), other foreign-born |
| Nationality | Country of origin |
| Institution | Which of the 11 state facilities |
| Date | Year of sterilization recommendation |
| Diagnosis | "Feeblemindedness," "insanity," "epilepsy," etc. |
| IQ/Mental age | Intelligence quotient assessments |
| Consent | Whether documented |
| Parental status | Whether individual had children |
| Legal provision | Legal basis for sterilization |

### Critical Limitation: Race/Ethnicity Coding
The original forms **did not systematically record race or ethnicity**. Researchers used proxies:
- **Latino identification:** 1980 US Census Spanish surname list (high sensitivity/specificity for mid-20th-century California; 88% of Spanish-surnamed individuals in 1950 CA were of Mexican descent)
- **Asian identification:** Birthplace data (Chinese, Indian, Japanese, Korean, Filipino-born)
- Authors acknowledge these as "imperfect criteria"

### Analytic Samples (varied by study)
| Study | N | Period | Exclusions |
|-------|---|--------|-----------|
| Novak et al. 2018 (Latino) | 17,362 | 1920-1945 | 490 records missing institution/gender/age |
| Stern et al. 2023 (Asian) | 13,499 | 1920-1945 | 4,282 missing nativity; 32 missing sex |
| Stern et al. 2017 (Survivors) | 19,498 | 1919-1952 | 497 with inadequate data |

### Known Gaps
- Not all recommendations resulted in actual sterilizations
- Sterilizations in prisons documented separately (not included)
- Private facilities and county hospitals not included
- Differential missing data by gender (26% women vs. 22% men missing nativity)
- Some periods better documented than others

## 6. Ethical Considerations

### Privacy and Living Survivors
- **Estimated 831 survivors alive as of 2016** (average age 87.9 years)
- California's **AB 1764** (2021) established the Forced or Involuntary Sterilization Compensation Program with explicit confidentiality provisions — applications are "completely confidential," records protected by HIPAA
- Individual sterilization records involve deeply sensitive personal health information
- Using this data without consent conflicts with the spirit of the reparations program

### Scholarly Consensus on Teaching with Eugenics Data
- "Teaching the work of eugenicists without contextualization or critique perpetuates their legacy" (CBE—Life Sciences Education)
- Historical and modern-day ableism must be openly discussed alongside racism
- Must avoid presenting victims solely through the lens of victimization

### Trauma-Informed Pedagogy Requirements
- **Content warnings** before each lesson with sensitive material
- **Opt-out alternatives** for students who may be triggered
- Students from affected communities (disabled, Latinx, Indigenous) may be in the classroom
- Establish explicit community norms for emotional conversations
- Do not insist students from affected communities participate in triggering activities

### For High School Curriculum Specifically
- Students need foundational understanding before encountering victimization data
- Teachers must be prepared to facilitate difficult conversations
- IRB principles (informed consent, beneficence, justice) still apply to classroom projects
- Framework should center survivors and descendants, not perpetrators
- Connect to broader data justice, disability justice, and reproductive justice

### Key Ethical Framework
Any educational use should:
1. Center survivors and descendants, not perpetrators or data curiosity
2. Acknowledge ongoing harm and living survivors
3. Use trauma-informed pedagogy with content warnings and opt-outs
4. Never treat victims as data points divorced from human dignity
5. Connect to contemporary data justice issues

## 7. Published Summary Statistics Available for Jupyter Notebooks

Since the raw data isn't public, here are the **extractable statistics from open-access papers** that students could work with:

### From Novak et al. 2018 — Racial Disparities

**Incidence Rate Ratios (Poisson regression):**

| Comparison | IRR | 95% CI |
|-----------|-----|--------|
| Latino men vs. non-Latino men | 1.23 | 1.15–1.31 |
| Latina women vs. non-Latina women | 1.59 | 1.48–1.70 |

- Figure 1 contains LOESS-smoothed sterilization rate curves by gender × ethnicity (1920-1945) — data points could be extracted
- Supplementary Tables A & B contain detailed regression coefficients

### From Stern et al. 2017 — Survivor Demographics

**Age at sterilization (among 2016 survivors):**

| Age Group | N | % |
|-----------|---|---|
| <12 years | 3 | 0.4% |
| 12-14 years | 137 | 16% |
| 15-17 years | 427 | 51% |
| 18-19 years | 113 | 14% |
| ≤17 years total | — | 68.1% |

**Estimated 2016 survivors:** 831 total (511 women, 320 men)

**Temporal trends:**
- 1920-1926: Higher rates for men than women
- Post-1926: Reversed — women sterilized at higher rates
- Peak period: 1945-1949 (48.5% of survivors sterilized in this 5-year window)

### From Stern et al. 2023 — Asian Immigrants

| Comparison | IRR | 95% CI |
|-----------|-----|--------|
| Asian-born women vs. US-born women | 2.00 | 1.61–2.48 |
| Asian-born men vs. US-born men (overall) | 0.95 | 0.83–1.10 |
| Asian-born men pre-1933 | 1.31 | 1.09–1.59 |
| Asian-born men post-1933 | 0.73 | 0.59–0.89 |

**Sample composition:** US-born: 10,909 (80.8%); Other foreign-born: 2,303 (17.1%); Asian-born: 287 (2.1%)

**Detailed tables available:** Tables 1-5 with population demographics, institutionalized population by nativity, sterilization demographics, and stratified regressions

### From Lutz Kaelber — State-by-State Totals
Available at [uvm.edu/~lkaelber/eugenics/](https://www.uvm.edu/~lkaelber/eugenics/):
- Number of sterilizations per state
- Time periods of programs
- Could be scraped/manually entered for comparative analysis across states

### Jupyter Notebook Ideas
Students could:
1. **Reproduce disparity analysis** using published IRRs and CIs — visualize racial/gender disparities
2. **Life table analysis** — recreate survivor estimates using published age-at-sterilization distributions
3. **Temporal trend visualization** — plot gender reversal in sterilization rates over time
4. **State comparison** — compile Kaelber's state totals into a dataset for geographic analysis
5. **Institutional analysis** — compare sterilization patterns across the 11 California institutions using published breakdowns
6. **Confidence interval interpretation** — teach statistical inference using the published IRRs

## Implications for Our Final Project

### The Data Reality
We cannot use individual-level sterilization records — they're restricted for good reason (living survivors, HIPAA, reparations privacy). Our curriculum must work with **published aggregate statistics** from the open-access AJPH papers.

### What Students CAN Work With
1. Published IRRs and confidence intervals showing racial/gender disparities
2. Age distributions and temporal trends from the papers' tables
3. State-by-state totals from Kaelber's compilation
4. Institutional breakdowns from the papers
5. Census denominator data (publicly available) combined with published sterilization rates

### What This Means for the Curriculum
The *restriction itself* is a data justice lesson — why can't students access this data? Who decides? What are the tensions between transparency/accountability and survivor privacy? This directly connects to our Data 4AC themes about power, access, and the ethics of data about marginalized communities.
